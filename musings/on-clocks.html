on clocks

a unified epoch and the problem of now

2026-03

The early web had a charming habit of broadcasting the host's local time in the footer — a small proof-of-life, a heartbeat. Visitor counters did the same thing for presence: someone was here, the clock ticked, the number incremented. These widgets were not useful. They were honest — a site saying, plainly, I exist in time too.

The footer of this site carries something in that tradition: a live readout of Unix time alongside estimated block heights for Bitcoin, Ethereum, and Solana. The Unix figure is exact. The chain figures carry a tilde — approximate, derived from genesis constants and average interval assumptions. I want to linger on why that tilde matters, and what it costs to remove it.

each chain is a clock

A blockchain does not merely record transactions — it records order. The chain is a clock in the strict sense: it defines a sequence of states, each causally after the last. But the rate at which this clock ticks, and what it means for a tick to be final, varies wildly.

Bitcoin's clock ticks roughly every ten minutes, but this is a probabilistic average maintained by difficulty adjustment. Any given block could arrive in thirty seconds or forty minutes. Finality is social convention layered over probability — six confirmations is not a protocol rule, it is a risk tolerance. The chain's answer to "what time is it" is: block 906,370, and we're fairly sure.

Ethereum post-Merge ticks every exactly twelve seconds by protocol. Slots are deterministic; the clock does not drift. Finality arrives two epochs out (~6.4 minutes) through attestation aggregation — a committee of validators vouching, collectively, that this is the canonical state. The answer is more precise, but it is still consensus, not measurement.

Solana's clock is something else. Proof of History encodes the passage of time directly into the ledger — a verifiable delay function hashing forward, producing a sequence that proves elapsed computation. Slots run at a nominal 400 milliseconds. In practice, with skipped slots, the average is closer to 460ms. The chain races. Its answer to "what time is it" arrives before you finish asking.

the rosetta stone problem

When you want to say "this on-chain event happened at unix time T," you are performing a cross-domain join. The chain knows its slot number. Unix knows wall clock time. Translating between them requires a bridge: either an on-chain oracle attesting to wall time, or the external assumption that you know approximately when block zero was produced and what the average interval has been since.

The latter is what the footer does. It is not wrong — it is approximately right, and the approximation is stable over long windows. Bitcoin's difficulty adjustment keeps the ten-minute average honest. Ethereum's slot time is a protocol constant. Solana drifts more, but remains bounded. The tilde is honest accounting for the residual error in this translation.

What you cannot do, without an oracle or external attestation, is know the exact current block from wall time alone — because you don't know the exact propagation delays, fork history, and validator behavior between genesis and now. The estimate is a projection, not a lookup.

unix as ground truth

It's worth pausing on the assumption buried in all of this: that Unix time is the neutral substrate, the common reference frame everything else gets mapped to. This is mostly true and occasionally wrong. Unix time is coordinated via NTP, which is itself a distributed protocol with its own failure modes. Leap seconds introduce discontinuities that different systems handle differently. UTC is a political construct maintained by agreement among national metrology institutes.

There is no global now. This is not a blockchain problem — it is a physics problem. Special relativity tells us that simultaneity is frame-dependent. For all practical purposes on a single planet, the speed-of-light discrepancies are negligible. But the principle holds: "now" is always a local concept, and consensus about it is a social achievement, not a natural fact.

Blockchains make this explicit. Each chain builds its own notion of order and calls it time. The interesting question is not which chain's clock is correct — they all are, within their own frames — but what it costs, and what guarantees you can make, when you try to join them.

toward a unified epoch

A unified cross-chain epoch is not a single canonical timestamp shared by all chains. That would require global coordination, which defeats the point. It is something softer: a common reference against which each chain's local time can be approximately translated, with explicit uncertainty bounds.

Unix serves this role today, imperfectly. Genesis timestamps and average block intervals are the conversion factors. The uncertainty compounds with chain age and variability. For most purposes — logging, coordination between off-chain systems, human communication — this is more than adequate. For on-chain logic that must agree across chains, it is not enough without oracles.

The footer will keep ticking. The tildes will stay. I find something clarifying about a display that marks its own approximation — a small reminder that every clock is a model, every timestamp an agreement, and the question of now is always worth asking again.
