Data sovereignty, proof without disclosure

On healthcare records, ESG attestation, and the geometry of trust

2026-03

Two problems that look unrelated share the same structure. In one, a patient wants researchers to use her health data without being able to identify her. In the other, an investor wants to know whether a company's sustainability score reflects what the company actually does, or just how well it has learned to fill out forms. Both problems are about the gap between a claim and the underlying reality — and about who gets to verify that gap is closed.

Zero-knowledge proofs close that gap mathematically. Append-only multi-party ledgers close it institutionally. Neither is sufficient alone. Together, they describe something that does not yet exist at scale but is not far off: a world where sensitive data can be made useful without being made visible, and where composite scores can be made trustworthy without being made opaque.

The proof primitive

Classical authentication proves knowledge of a secret by revealing it, or at least a transformation of it. A zero-knowledge proof does something stranger: it convinces a verifier that a statement is true while revealing nothing beyond that fact. Three properties are required simultaneously — completeness (true statements can always be proven), soundness (false statements cannot, except with probability approaching zero), and zero-knowledge (nothing leaks beyond validity).

The cave intuition: a prover claims to know a secret passphrase that opens a door between two tunnels. The verifier waits outside, calls out which tunnel to exit from. The prover, knowing the passphrase, can always comply. After enough rounds, the probability of sustained lucky guessing is negligible — yet the verifier has learned nothing about the passphrase itself. Only: the prover almost certainly knows it.

Interactive proofs like this are impractical for most deployed systems, so the field moved to non-interactive variants. zk-SNARKs collapse the challenge-response into a single message using cryptographic hash functions and a common reference string; they are succinct and fast to verify but often require a trusted setup whose secrecy must be maintained. zk-STARKs remove the trusted setup (transparent parameters, hence the T), are post-quantum resistant, and produce larger proofs. PLONK and related families offer universal setups reusable across different circuits. All three show up in Ethereum L2s, private transaction schemes like Zcash, and emerging identity infrastructure.

Applied to storage, ZKPs handle the authentication layer: a provider can verify that you know a password without you transmitting it, and without the provider storing anything that could be stolen and replayed. The data layer is handled separately by client-side encryption — your files are encrypted before leaving your device, so the provider's servers hold ciphertext they cannot read regardless of legal compulsion or breach.

Healthcare: the patient as prover

The healthcare application makes the power of ZKPs concrete. A researcher wants to know whether a patient cohort has undergone a specific treatment. Traditional approaches require either full record disclosure (too much) or institutional de-identification (imperfect, reversible, and not patient-controlled). A ZKP approach inverts this: the patient proves to the researcher that the relevant conditions are met — "I have undergone treatment X," "I satisfy criterion Y for this study" — without the researcher ever seeing the underlying record.

The patient is the prover. The researcher is the verifier. What the researcher receives is a proof, not data. The proof is machine-verifiable and cryptographically binding. The record stays with the patient.

Recent literature pairs this with permissioned blockchains for consent management: the patient's consent to share specific attributes is logged on-chain, revocable in real time, and auditable by regulators without exposing the underlying data. Off-chain encrypted storage (IPFS or similar) holds the actual records; the chain holds only hashes and consent state. Layer-2 rollups (zk-Rollups specifically) provide the throughput that health record systems require.

Truveta and the alternative model

Truveta's approach to the same problem is instructive precisely because it is different. They aggregate EHR data from over 130 million patients through institutional de-identification and data use agreements — not patient-verifiable cryptographic proofs. This works for aggregate analytics at scale: researchers get rich, representative cohorts without encountering individual records. The tradeoff is that patients have no cryptographic guarantee about how their data is used, and the de-identification process, however rigorous, is a statistical not a mathematical guarantee.

Neither approach is universally better. Truveta's model is deployable today with existing infrastructure and regulatory frameworks. The ZKP model is closer to patient sovereignty in the fullest sense but requires infrastructure that is still maturing. The interesting question is whether they converge — whether patient-controlled cryptographic proofs become the interface through which aggregate analytics pipelines are fed, rather than a competing paradigm.

ESG: the company as prover — and why that fails

The ESG problem is structurally similar but the prover is the wrong party. Under current methodology, the company proves to the rating agency that its practices meet certain standards — by self-reporting. The rating agency aggregates these self-reports, normalizes them against peers, and produces a score. The investor trusts the score.

This is a three-party trust chain where the entity with the most to gain from favorable scores controls the primary input to those scores. The mathematical analogy is a prover who gets to write their own challenge. The proof is not zero-knowledge — it is not even a proof. It is a disclosure.

Empirical research makes the consequences concrete. Benuzzi et al. (2025) decompose year-over-year ESG score changes into three components: a company's own changes in disclosed data (OC), changes in how peer companies disclose (PC), and the expansion of the rating universe as new firms enter the sample (NC). Across three sectors from 2012 to 2021, less than 45% of total absolute variation is attributable to OC. The remaining 55% is methodology artifact — the score moves because of who joined the universe or how peers shifted, not because of what the company did.

The NC effect alone is striking: when smaller, less-disclosed firms enter the Refinitiv universe, they cluster at the bottom of the distribution, mechanically lifting all incumbents' percentile ranks. One machinery-sector company in the paper gained 17.2 points from this effect alone over nine years — points that had nothing to do with its actual environmental or social practices.

A score that improved. Nothing else did.

The Performance Ratio (PR) alternative proposed by Benuzzi et al. fixes the aggregation: under PR, scores move when a company's own values change, not when the peer universe shifts. But PR still accepts company self-disclosure as the input. The proof is cleaner; the prover is still the interested party.

The architecture that follows

The structural fix is to change who supplies the data. Companies are not the parties with the most direct knowledge of their own sustainability practices — their workers are, their suppliers are, their energy providers are, the communities adjacent to their operations are. These parties have less incentive to distort and more direct observational access. Making them the attestors rather than passive subjects changes the information architecture entirely.

An append-only public ledger is the record-keeping mechanism this requires. Not because blockchain is magical, but because the guarantee matters: no single administrator should be able to revise historical attestations after the fact. Refinitiv currently can, and does, revise historical ESG scores — the paper documents a five-year revision window. A ledger where writes are permanent and provenance is cryptographically attributable makes retroactive manipulation structurally impossible rather than merely prohibited.

On public weights

The usual objection to transparent scoring is that public weights invite gaming. If participants know that worker sentiment carries 15% weight, won't they optimize for the metric rather than the underlying reality?

This concern is correct for single-source systems. In a company-controlled disclosure regime, the company can hit a metric without changing the underlying phenomenon — reframe a policy document, adjust a reporting boundary, commission a favorable audit. The measure and the reality decouple.

In a multi-party attestation system, the concern inverts. Improving a worker sentiment score requires improving conditions workers actually experience, because workers are the ones reporting. There is no substitute for the underlying reality when the party with direct access to that reality is the one providing the proof. The gaming and the genuine improvement become indistinguishable — which is precisely the point.

Public weights in a multi-party system don't enable gaming. They make the system's priorities legible. Workers who know their attestations carry real weight have a reason to attest carefully. Companies who know which dimensions matter have specific, verifiable targets to hit. The feedback loop runs in the right direction.

What the system does not require

It does not require full corporate transparency. A company need not disclose its energy procurement contracts if the utility is independently attesting to the delivered energy mix. Competitive confidentiality and score accuracy coexist because the information surface has multiple independent access points.

It does not require perfect attestors. Individual workers will sometimes misreport; suppliers have their own incentives. The system's integrity rests on aggregate consistency across independent sources, not individual honesty. Coordinated misreporting across independent attestors is both expensive to maintain and structurally visible — divergence between simultaneous attestations is anomalous and detectable without anyone actively investigating. The system surfaces inconsistencies passively.

It does not require resolving every methodological debate about what sustainability means. Weights are parameters subject to deliberation and empirical calibration. Multiple composite views of the same attestation data can coexist. What is non-negotiable is that the underlying record is public, the formula is auditable, and no single party controls either.

The convergence

Healthcare and ESG are both cases where the entity whose behavior is being evaluated has historically controlled the primary evidentiary channel. Patients' health data flows through providers and insurers before it reaches researchers. Companies' sustainability data flows through their own disclosure teams before it reaches rating agencies. In both cases, the solution involves redistributing epistemic authority — giving the parties with direct observational access a mechanism to contribute that access to the record directly, without requiring the intermediary's cooperation or the subject's consent.

Zero-knowledge proofs are the tool that makes this redistribution legible: they allow a claim to be verified without the underlying data being exposed. The append-only ledger is the infrastructure that makes it durable: it ensures the record of attestations cannot be quietly revised after the fact. Together they describe a class of systems where accuracy does not depend on the honesty of the most interested party.

We are not there yet. The healthcare ZKP infrastructure is largely in research and pilots. The ESG multi-party attestation architecture exists as a proposal, not a deployed system. But the underlying mathematics is not speculative, the institutional logic is sound, and the failures of the current arrangements are well-documented. What remains, in both domains, is the institutional will to prefer accuracy over convenience — which is, historically, a lagging indicator.

A note on method

The discussion that produced these notes moved between cloud storage tiers, ZKP mechanics, Truveta's data model, HIPAA constraints, ESG methodology critique, ledger design, and incentive theory — not as isolated topics but as facets of a single question about what it means to verify something without exposing it. The following exchange captures something of that texture.

Zero-knowledge options — these are pretty cool. Could we get a short description on how zero-knowledge circuits work in attesting the authenticity of the encapsulated data without "peering in"? Quite a powerful subfield. One application that comes to mind is sharing medical patient data with researchers — unsure if [Truveta is] utilizing zero-knowledge proofs as it might be a bit overkill for the business requirement, although it could be an interesting approach — a methodology by which patients can comfortably share their sensitive HIPAA data with researchers, potentially get paid for it, and sleep well knowing their information is not being utilized maliciously.

The key insight is that ZKPs replace brand-based promises with mathematical guarantees. You don't have to trust the provider to keep data safe — it is safe by virtue of the implementation. The authentication problem is subtle: the provider doesn't store your password, so how do they verify it's correct? Answer: a zero-knowledge proof lets the provider verify you know the key without requiring you to reveal it.

The proxy measure is decoupled from the underlying phenomenon — in this case, they're quite literally coupled. ESG reports and the participants in the system are interconnected in terms of outcomes. There is no benefit to participants to introduce negative outcomes to the system. They are incentivized to participate positively — reporting the truth, i.e., the point behind the ESG system.

Goodhart's Law has a prerequisite that's often glossed over: it applies when the proxy measure is decoupled from the underlying phenomenon. "When a measure becomes a target it ceases to be a good measure" — but only when you can hit the measure without moving the underlying. If the measure is constructed such that you can't move it without moving the underlying, Goodhart doesn't bite. Multi-party attestation with public weights is specifically designed to close that gap.

The design implication is that you probably want attestor independence metrics to be part of the public information surface from the start — not just what attestors report, but how correlated their reports are with the company's own disclosures, which is itself a red flag signal. Yes — company disclosures and the data the respective ESG reports were composed from should be disclosed transparently. It's all right for companies to not disclose everything — that's in their rights. What's not okay is individuals not being aware of how the score is composed, or whether they can verify its validity.

That property — that accuracy doesn't depend on the cooperation of the entity being scored — is what distinguishes this from every existing ESG methodology, including the Performance Ratio the paper proposes. The company's non-participation cannot suppress the signal, because the company is not the sole input.

The system doesn't break if a company decides to game it, because the company is not the sole input. (That's a, uh, great closing line — kidding.)
